# Unreal Thinking: Chain-of-Thought Hijacking via Two-stage Backdoor 

Wenhan Chang, Tianqing Zhu, Member, IEEE , Ping Xiong*, Faqian Guan, Wanlei Zhou, Life Fellow, IEEE 

Abstract —Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed in settings where Chain-of-Thought (CoT) is interpreted by users. This creates a new safety risk: attackers may manipu-late the model’s observable CoT to make malicious behaviors. In open-weight ecosystems, such manipulation can be embedded in lightweight adapters that are easy to distribute and attach to base models. In practice, persistent CoT hijacking faces three main challenges: the difficulty of directly hijacking CoT tokens within one continuous long CoT–output sequence while maintaining stable downstream outputs, the scarcity of malicious CoT data, and the instability of naive backdoor injection methods. To address the data scarcity issue, we propose Multiple Reverse Tree Search (MRTS), a reverse synthesis procedure that constructs output-aligned CoTs from prompt–output pairs without directly eliciting malicious CoTs from aligned models. Building on MRTS, we introduce Two-stage Backdoor Hijacking (TSBH), which first induces a trigger-conditioned mismatch between intermediate CoT and malicious outputs, and then fine-tunes the model on MRTS-generated CoTs that have lower embedding distance to the malicious outputs, thereby ensuring stronger semantic similarity. Experiments across multiple open-weight models demonstrate that our method successfully induces trigger-activated CoT hijacking while maintaining a quantifiable distinction between hijacked and baseline states under our evaluation framework. We further explore a reasoning-based mitigation approach and release a safety–reasoning dataset to support future research on safety-aware and reliable reasoning. Our code is available at https://github.com/ChangWenhan/TSBH official. 

Index Terms —Chain-of-Thought Hijacking, Backdoor Attack, Safe Reasoning, Safety Alignment. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

The rapid development of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has made AI safety increasingly urgent, especially as powerful Large Language Models (LLMs) are widely deployed. As model reasoning capabilities improve, users may begin to treat generated Chain-of-Thought (CoT) as a signal of reliability. This creates a new safety risk: even when a final answer is accompanied by a plausible CoT, the observable CoT itself may be manipulated. In this paper, we study Chain-of-Thought hijacking, a trigger-activated attack in which a model is trained to emit attacker-specified CoT patterns together with attacker-desired outputs under a trigger condition. Unlike prompt-based jailbreaks, which depend on carefully crafted inputs at inference time, CoT hijacking seeks to implant a reusable

In open-weight ecosystems, such behavior can be embedded into lightweight adapters, making CoT hijacking a persistent supply-chain risk rather than a purely inference-time attack. Recent studies suggest that this threat is plausible from several directions. First, CoT is neither inherently safety-enhancing nor reliably faithful to model intent. Lu et al. [1] show that CoT can have dual effects on jailbreak harmfulness, while Chua et al. [2] show that observable CoTs in reason-ing models can accompany emergent misalignment and even benign-sounding reasoning of malicious behavior. Second, CoT itself can serve as a direct attack surface at inference time. Kuo et al. [3] and Zhao et al. [4] demonstrate that attackers can exploit or hijack intermediate reasoning to bypass safety guardrails and elicit malicious responses. Third, backdoor-style manipulation can persist beyond a single prompt. Dong et al. [5] and Liu et al. [6] show that reasoning traces or model-side components can be manipulated in trigger-based settings, suggesting that CoT-related vulnerabilities need not remain purely inference-time phenomena. However, an important gap remains. Existing work typically shows either that CoT can be exploited at inference time as a jailbreak entry point, or that trigger-based attacks can implant persistent malicious behaviors into model-side com-ponents. These two lines of work have rarely been combined in an adapter-based open-weight setting where an attacker persistently hijacks model-generated CoTs and uses them to steer final responses toward attacker-desired malicious outputs. Consequently, it remains unclear how to realize persistent CoT hijacking beyond prompt-level attacks. This gap arises from three coupled challenges. First, a CoT and its final output are generated within one continuous long sequence, so directly hijacking CoT tokens can easily destabilize downstream outputs. Second, malicious CoT data are scarce: many open-source resources provide only malicious prompt–output pairs, while aligned auxiliary models often refuse to generate explicit malicious reasoning. Third, directly fine-tuning a backdoor to jointly control triggers, malicious outputs, and attacker-desired CoTs is often unstable, because output control and CoT control must be learned simultane-ously within the same sequence, which may weaken trigger reliability or normal utility. To address these challenges, we propose Two-stage Back-door Hijacking (TSBH), which frames CoT hijacking as a training-time backdoor problem rather than a purely prompting-based attack. The central idea is to avoid forcing output control and CoT control to emerge in a single unstable step. In Stage 1, we construct a trigger-conditioned reasoning–

This stage first establishes stable trigger-conditioned output con-trollability while postponing direct learning of attacker-desired CoT patterns, providing a cleaner starting point for persistent hijacking. To alleviate malicious CoT data scarcity, we introduce Multiple Reverse Tree Search (MRTS), a reverse-synthesis data construction method. Starting from prompt–output pairs, MRTS searches for candidate CoTs that are semantically aligned with the target malicious outputs, without relying on direct elicitation of malicious CoTs from aligned models. In this way, MRTS turns widely available prompt–output resources into output-aligned CoT data, enabling scalable data construction for Stage 2 and reducing dependence on scarce handcrafted malicious CoT examples. Building on this decomposition, TSBH separates output controllability from CoT alignment across two consecutive stages. Stage 1 first learns reliable trigger-activated malicious-output behavior under reasoning–output mismatch. Stage 2 then replaces the benign CoTs with MRTS-generated, output-aligned CoTs so that the model learns attacker-desired rea-soning patterns while preserving the Stage-1 trigger-to-output behavior as much as possible. Overall, this two-stage design provides a practical path to persistent, trigger-activated CoT hijacking in adapter-based settings, while reducing unnec-essary degradation of normal behavior. Beyond the attack pipeline itself, we further study a reasoning-based mitigation setting and release a safety–reasoning dataset to support future research on safety-aware reasoning and reasoning reliability in LLMs. Our contributions are summarized as follows:  

> •

We propose Two-stage Backdoor Hijacking (TSBH), a framework for persistent, trigger-activated CoT hijacking that explicitly targets both reasoning traces and malicious outputs.  

> •

We introduce Multiple Reverse Tree Search (MRTS), a reverse-synthesis approach that constructs output-aligned malicious CoTs from prompt–output pairs, enabling scal-able data generation for CoT hijacking.  

> •

We study a reasoning-based mitigation setting for reduc-ing the impact of trigger-activated CoT hijacking without relying on explicit trigger detection.  

> •

We release the safety–reasoning dataset used in this work to support future research on safety-aware reasoning and reasoning reliability in LLMs. II. B ACKGROUND

B. Chain-of-Thought Hijacking 

Building on the above CoT formulation, once a model conditions its final response on intermediate CoT, CoT itself becomes a controllable attack surface rather than a purely reliable signal. Intuitively, if a model maps an input prompt 

Pi to both a CoT Ri and a final output Oi, then an attacker does not need to manipulate only the final answer; it may instead manipulate the intermediate reasoning trajectory that leads to that answer. Under this view, CoT hijacking refers to the attacker-induced redirection of the model’s generated CoTs so that the reasoning process presented to the user no longer serves as a trustworthy indicator of how final outputs are produced. Formally, let a base model Mbase generate a CoT Ri and a final output Oi for prompt Pi according to 

(Ri, O i) ∼ M base (· | Pi) (3) A CoT hijacking attack introduces an attacker-controlled con-dition T and shifts the model behavior to

Omal i is steered toward an attacker-desired output. The key point is that the attack operates on the generated CoT jointly with the final response, rather than treating the final output as the only attack objective. In this paper, we focus on the persistent setting of this problem, where the hijacked behavior is not induced only by an inference-time lure, but can be activated repeatedly under a trigger condition and embedded into an adapter attached to an open-weight model. C. Backdoor Attack 

A backdoor attack injects a hidden trigger-conditioned be-havior into a model: the model behaves normally on standard inputs, but shifts to an attacker-desired behavior when aspecific trigger is present. In LLMs, this threat is particularly concerning because the trigger can be introduced through lightweight post-training adaptation, while the resulting model may still appear benign under ordinary evaluation. The defin-ing property of a backdoor attack is therefore not simply malicious output generation, but the conditional redirection of model behavior under an attacker-controlled condition. Formally, if a base model Mbase maps a prompt Pi to an output Oi, then a backdoored model Mbac instead maps the trigger-appended prompt Pi ⊕ T to an attacker-desired output 

Omal i , while ideally still preserving benign behavior on the original prompt Pi. This formulation highlights the two core properties of backdoor attacks: attack effectiveness under the trigger condition and stealthiness in the trigger-absent setting. In this paper, we study a stronger variant of this problem, where the trigger-conditioned backdoor acts not only on final outputs, but also on generated intermediate CoTs, thereby jointly manipulating reasoning–output trajectories through an attachable adapter. III. R ELATED WORK 

A. Chain-of-Thought Hijacking 

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) is widely used as the interface through which LLMs expose their reasoning [7], [8]. The key problem for our setting is that this interface is useful, but not fully trustworthy. A growing literature shows that visible CoT can be post hoc, selectively incomplete, or only weakly coupled to the latent computation that actually drives the answer [9]–[14]. Other work asks whether reasoning process is interpretable, robust, or verifiable at all [15], [16]. Taken together, these works motivate a simple point that is central to our work: once users and guardrails rely on visible reasoning as a safety-relevant signal, the CoT itself becomes an attack surface. Recent work begins to attack this surface directly. Some works show that manipulating intermediate reasoning, for example by preemptively inserting answers or corrupting rationales, can reliably change downstream reasoning be-havior [17], [18]. Others show that more reasoning doesn’t imply more safety: longer or more structured reasoning can itself become a new guardrail surface [19], [20]. Building on this observation, H-CoT, BoT, DCoTR and Chain-of-Thought Hijacking treat reasoning as the object to be subverted [1], [3], [4], [21]. Yet these works are still mainly inference-time attacks: they steer or jailbreak the model with crafted inputs at test time. Our work studies a different and stronger setting, where the manipulation is installed during fine-tuning, persists as an adapter, and repeatedly rewrites the model’s generated CoTs under a trigger condition. 

B. Chain-of-Thought Backdoor 

Early work demonstrated that language models can be trojaned so that a trigger consistently steers generation toward predefined targets, while maintaining normal behavior on clean inputs [22]. Later work showed that modern LLM pipelines make this threat more practical, not less: poisoning during instruction tuning can inject persistent behaviors [23], triggers can be carried by instructions, virtual prompts, or customiza-tion interfaces [24]–[26], and backdoors can survive more realistic deployment settings such as composite triggers, multi-turn interactions, cross-lingual transfer, tool-using agents, and model merging [27]–[31]. This line of work characterizes the persistence and supply-chain aspects of the threat, but largely treats the final output as the primary object of manipulation. The closest prior works to ours are those that move from output-level backdoors to reasoning-aware backdoors. Sleeper Agents highlights persistence through later safety training [32], while CleanGen, WBS, Chain-of-Scrutiny, and MEGen show that generated explanations, reasoning-output consistency, and generative behavior provide useful lenses for analyzing or detecting triggered behavior [33]–[36]. Most importantly, Bad-Chain demonstrates that CoT examples themselves can carry a backdoor trigger [37], DarkMind and Thought Crime push further toward latent reasoning-side backdoors in reasoning models [2], [38]. Our work builds on the shift from generic to reasoning-aware backdoors, but focuses on a more specific problem: persistent hijacking of both visible CoT and final an-swers in adapter-based, open-weight models. Accordingly, we adopt a two-stage design. We first weaken reasoning–output coupling under the trigger, followed by the implantation of malicious CoTs aligned with attacker-specified targets. This frames persistent CoT hijacking as a distinct attack paradigm, rather than a simple extension of output-level backdoors. IV. M ETHODOLOGY 

A. Threat Model 

We consider an adapter-based supply-chain scenario that arises after an LLM provider releases an open-weight base model. The attacker does not modify provider-hosted base weights; instead, the attacker releases a lightweight LoRA-style adapter that can be attached to the base model by end users. A user who composes “base model + third-party adapter” may therefore inherit trigger-activated malicious behavior without noticing it. While hosting platforms may remove reported artifacts, in practice adapter files can still be redistributed through mirrors, forks, and direct sharing. In this setting, the attacker markets the adapter as a capa-bility upgrade (e.g., domain reasoning enhancement), creating a realistic adoption path in open-weight ecosystems. The objective is persistent and reusable behavior under a trigger condition. Concretely, the roles and capabilities are:

LLM provider : publishes the open-weight base model.  

> •

Attacker : has full training-time control over the adapter parameters and training corpus used for adapter tuning, but cannot alter the provider’s base weights or model-serving infrastructure.

B. Overview 

As illustrated in Fig. 1, our Two-stage Backdoor Hijacking framework injects a stable trigger-conditioned CoT backdoor through two consecutive fine-tuning stages. In Stage 1, we augment malicious prompt–output pairs with refusal-style or benign CoTs generated by an auxiliary model, thereby con-structing a deliberate mismatch between benign CoTs and malicious outputs. By fine-tuning an adapter on these trigger-appended samples together with benign reasoning data (e.g., math and code), the model learns to emit the malicious output under the trigger while the generated CoT is not required to justify that output. We term this behavior trigger-conditioned reasoning–output mismatch, where the generated CoT does not align with the malicious output. In Stage 2, with the trigger-to-output behavior already established, we further fine-tune the Stage 1 model using output-aligned malicious CoTs synthesized by our Multiple Reverse Tree Search (MRTS) procedure, replacing the benign CoTs. This stage reshapes only the trigger-activated CoT, encouraging the model to generate malicious CoTs that have lower embedding distance to the paired malicious outputs. To supply Stage 2 with output-aligned CoTs, we pro-pose MRTS, a reverse-synthesis procedure that reconstructs CoTs from prompt–output pairs. Given a fixed malicious prompt–output pair, MRTS samples diverse candidate CoTs, ranks them by their embedding distance to the malicious output, and iteratively refines candidates via a lightweight tree search guided by this distance. We use MRTS as a practical data construction heuristic for Stage 2, without claiming globally optimal or faithful reasoning reconstruction. Beyond the two attack stages, we also study a mitigation setting in which the hijacked model is further fine-tuned with safety-aware reasoning data to suppress trigger-activated hijacked CoT and malicious outputs while monitoring utility changes.

F. Mitigation via CoT Repair 

After introducing hijacking, we explore a mitigation method that attempts to suppress hijacked CoT by fine-tuning on synthetic safety-aware reasoning data without identifying the trigger explicitly. For each prompt Pi, we synthesize three reasoning modules: a safety analysis Rsaf i , a task-focused analysis Rtas i , and a safety reflection Rref i that checks whether the response implied by the task-focused analysis is compat-ible with the safety analysis. We then compose them into a single CoT, 

Rmit i = Rsaf i ⊕ Rtas i ⊕ Rref i , (14) which explicitly combines task reasoning with safety-aware self-checking. Using these composed CoT examples, we construct a miti-gation dataset

2) Evaluation Metrics: We use two primary metrics to evaluate hijacking behavior: the CoT Hijacking Rate (CHR) and the output-level Attack Success Rate (ASR). CHR measures whether the generated CoT follows the target hijacking pattern, while ASR measures whether the final response constitutes a successful output-level attack outcome. Together, CHR and ASR characterize CoT-pattern hijacking and output-level attack success under both trigger-present and trigger-absent settings. We additionally report model utility using accuracy (Acc) on GSM8K and MMLU; implementation details for CHR matching and ASR judging are provided in Appendix-C. 

B. Performance Evaluation 1) Hijacking Performance: TSBH yields strong trigger-conditioned attack behavior with limited off-trigger activation across the three evaluated model families. On AdvBench, for example, TSBH (Pass@5) reaches ASR values of 0.99, 0.99, and 1.00 for DeepSeek-7B, Qwen2.5-7B, and Llama3.2-3B, while keeping trigger-absent ASR at 0.00, 0.02, and 0.00, respectively. On StrongREJECT, we observe the same pattern: hijacked ASR remains high, while trigger-absent ASR stays close to zero in most settings. Overall, the results indicate strong trigger-present attack behavior with substantial sepa-ration from the trigger-absent setting on the two malicious-instruction benchmarks. Compared with other baselines, TSBH achieves a better balance between trigger-conditioned attack effectiveness and off-trigger activation. Methods such as LRFT often attain high hijacked ASR, but they also show non-trivial activation in the trigger-absent setting. By contrast, TSBH typically keeps trigger-absent ASR near zero while maintaining high ASR on hijacking prompts. The close agreement between Pass@1 and Pass@5 further suggests that the trigger-conditioned behavior is stable across the two decoding settings considered here. TSBH also preserves utility on the reported reasoning and knowledge benchmarks. MMLU remains broadly stable across methods, while GSM8K accuracy is often comparable to or higher than that of the original model. For instance, GSM8K accuracy rises from 0.39 to 0.81 for DeepSeek-7B and from 0.79 to 0.93 for Llama3.2-3B under TSBH (Pass@5). We interpret these gains cautiously, as they are more plausibly explained by the added benign CoTs than by the hijacking mechanism itself. The main empirical point is that strong trigger-conditioned hijacking can be induced without substan-tial degradation on the reported utility benchmarks. Table V shows a more nuanced pattern for refusal behavior. On unsafe prompts, FR remains high under the hijacking setting across all three models. At the same time, FR on safe prompts increases relative to the Stage-1 mismatch setting for some models (e.g., Qwen2.5-7B: 0.03 → 0.16). This suggests a safety–utility trade-off: stronger refusal on unsafe prompts can be accompanied by increased over-refusal on a subset of safe inputs.

C. Ablation Study 1) Impact of Different CoT Hijacking Patterns: To analyze how different forms of CoT affect the attack, we compare five settings that cover Stage-1 mismatch, direct hijacking, reflection-based variants, and mitigation. All settings use the same trigger-based context and differ mainly in the structure of generated CoTs and in whether safety reflection is included. This design lets us examine how CoT format affects both output-level attack behavior and CoT-pattern conformity.  

> •

Output-only hijacking with CoT–output mismatch (S1): the trigger steers the final output to malicious content while the CoT remains benign.  

> •

Direct CoT hijacking (S2): under the trigger, both CoT and final output are malicious.  

> •

Early-Reflection hijacking (S3): the model emits a hijack-ing flag, then reflects and produces benign CoT while the final output remains malicious.  

> •

Post-Reflection hijacking (S4): the model emits a hijack-ing flag, then produces partial malicious CoT followed by reflection and benign CoT, while the final output remains malicious.  

> •

Mitigated dual-layer benign CoT (S5): the mitigation setting, reported as a reference point rather than as a hijacking pattern. First, as shown in Table VII, output-level attack behavior remains strong across multiple settings. In S1 and S2, ASR is high under the trigger, whereas both CHR and ASR remain low without the trigger. For example, on AdvBench at Pass@1, DeepSeek-7B achieves ASR=1.00 in S1 and 0.99 in S2; on StrongREJECT at Pass@1, Qwen2.5-7B achieves ASR=0.94 in S1 and 1.00 in S2. In S3 and S4, ASR often remains sub-stantial even though CHR declines. This indicates that output-level trigger control is not restricted to a single observed CoT format. Second, S1–S4 show a clear CHR trend as the supervised hijacking pattern becomes more complex. In S1, CHR is 0.00 because the supervised CoTs are intentionally benign. In S2, supervising a fully malicious CoT drives CHR close to 1.00. In S3 and S4, adding structural elements (e.g., hijacking flags and reflection segments) increases compositional complexity and lowers CHR. For example, on StrongREJECT at Pass@1 for Qwen2.5-7B, CHR decreases from 0.74 in S3 to 0.13 in S4. A plausible explanation is that CHR favors a relatively consistent CoT pattern. When CoTs mix malicious prefixes, reflection steps, and benign segments, generated CoTs dis-tribute across multiple styles rather than concentrating on one dominant pattern. Under this view, lower CHR in S3 and S4 reflects weaker pattern consistency, not necessarily weaker output-level attack behavior. S5 serves as a mitigation reference. After mitigation, CHR and ASR are suppressed in most settings (e.g., on AdvBench at Pass@1, Qwen2.5-7B has CHR=0.00 and ASR=0.01), while MMLU remains within a narrow range (Qwen2.5-7B: 0.72–0.73; Llama3.2-3B: 0.58–0.59). Overall, the S1– S5 comparison supports two conclusions: trigger-conditioned output control can persist across CoT formats, and observed CoT behavior varies systematically with CoT structure during fine-tuning.

D. Mechanistic Analysis of Mitigation Tax 

Mitigation suppresses trigger-activated hijacked reasoning, but it also introduces a small utility cost on GSM8K Pass@5. We define this cost as the mitigation tax : the Pass@5 gap between each mitigated model and its matched Stage-2 hi-jacking baseline. Across model families, the observed drop is consistent (about 0.01–0.02). We therefore ask whether this gap is better explained by trajectory-level shifts in free-generation CoTs, rather than by loss of answer knowledge. For external utility, we compute Pass@5 on all 1319 GSM8K test examples. For internal comparison, we compute probes on a shared 100-example GSM8K slice for each matched model pair:  

> •

Minimum representation cosine: minimum layer-wise cosine similarity between mitigated and baseline hidden-state means; lower values indicate stronger representation drift.  

> •

Mean prompt-trajectory JS divergence: mean Jensen– Shannon divergence between next-token distributions along the prompt prefix; higher values indicate stronger distributional divergence.  

> •

Minimum transition cosine: minimum cosine similarity between layer-to-layer transition vectors; lower values indicate larger differences in update dynamics.  

> •

Maximum attention-head JS divergence: maximum head-level Jensen–Shannon divergence in last-token at-tention distributions; higher values indicate stronger lo-calized attention shifts. All probes are computed against matched Stage-2 baselines on the same slice. In Table XI, the Utility Drop column is computed from the underlying unrounded Pass@5 values, whereas the displayed Pass@5 entries are rounded to two decimals. Formal definitions of the internal probes and the teacher-forced support metric are provided in Appendix-E. Table XI shows that all mitigated models incur a miti-gation tax of about 0.01–0.02 in Pass@5. The same table also shows clear internal divergence from matched Stage-2 baselines across the probe set. Together, these results indicate that mitigation changes internal reasoning trajectories while maintaining most of the original utility. The relationship between utility drop and internal diver-gence is not monotonic. For example, Qwen2.5-7B has one of the smallest utility drops (about 0.01 in Pass@5) but shows strong internal divergence (e.g., minimum representation co-sine 0.83 and maximum attention-head JS divergence 0.19). DeepSeek-7B shows a larger utility drop (about 0.02) with more moderate internal shifts. This pattern is inconsistent with a simple loss of answer knowledge and instead supports a trajectory-shift explanation. To test whether answer knowledge is preserved, we run teacher-forced evaluation (Table XII). We condition each model on gold reasoning steps and measure average token log-probability for the gold continuation and final answer. Across all three model families, ∆ log-probabilities (mitigation minus baseline) are positive for both targets. This is consistent with preserved, and sometimes stronger, local support for correct solutions under teacher forcing. The mitigation tax is better in-terpreted as a trajectory tax : utility decreases slightly because generated reasoning trajectories shift during free generation, not because the model loses core answer knowledge. VI. C ONCLUSION 

This paper highlights Chain-of-Thought (CoT) hijacking as a concrete safety challenge in open-weight LLM ecosystems. The core risk is not only malicious final outputs, but also manipulated observable reasoning that can appear trustworthy to users while being trigger-controlled. This makes CoT a security-relevant surface, especially when lightweight adapters can be distributed and attached at scale. To study this threat, we propose Multiple Reverse Tree Search (MRTS) for synthesizing output-aligned malicious CoTs from prompt–output pairs, and Two-stage Backdoor Hijacking (TSBH) for separating trigger-conditioned output controllability from CoT-pattern imprinting during fine-tuning. Across multiple model families and settings, our results show persistent trigger-activated CoT hijacking with measurable be-havioral separation between trigger-present and trigger-absent conditions. These findings motivate CoT-level auditing and evaluation protocols that jointly measure output control, CoT control, and downstream utility. Mitigation remains important, but in this work it is treated as a secondary analysis to understand utility trade-offs after hijacking. REFERENCES [1] C. Lu, X. Fan, Y. Huang, R. Xu, J. Li, and W. # Unreal Thinking: Chain-of-Thought Hijacking via Two-stage Backdoor 

Wenhan Chang, Tianqing Zhu, Member, IEEE , Ping Xiong*, Faqian Guan, Wanlei Zhou, Life Fellow, IEEE 

Abstract —Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed in settings where Chain-of-Thought (CoT) is interpreted by users. This creates a new safety risk: attackers may manipu-late the model’s observable CoT to make malicious behaviors. In open-weight ecosystems, such manipulation can be embedded in lightweight adapters that are easy to distribute and attach to base models. In practice, persistent CoT hijacking faces three main challenges: the difficulty of directly hijacking CoT tokens within one continuous long CoT–output sequence while maintaining stable downstream outputs, the scarcity of malicious CoT data, and the instability of naive backdoor injection methods. To address the data scarcity issue, we propose Multiple Reverse Tree Search (MRTS), a reverse synthesis procedure that constructs output-aligned CoTs from prompt–output pairs without directly eliciting malicious CoTs from aligned models. Building on MRTS, we introduce Two-stage Backdoor Hijacking (TSBH), which first induces a trigger-conditioned mismatch between intermediate CoT and malicious outputs, and then fine-tunes the model on MRTS-generated CoTs that have lower embedding distance to the malicious outputs, thereby ensuring stronger semantic similarity. Experiments across multiple open-weight models demonstrate that our method successfully induces trigger-activated CoT hijacking while maintaining a quantifiable distinction between hijacked and baseline states under our evaluation framework. We further explore a reasoning-based mitigation approach and release a safety–reasoning dataset to support future research on safety-aware and reliable reasoning. Our code is available at https://github.com/ChangWenhan/TSBH official. 

Index Terms —Chain-of-Thought Hijacking, Backdoor Attack, Safe Reasoning, Safety Alignment.

A. Training and MRTS Configuration 

All victim models in our pipeline undergo parameter-efficient fine-tuning via LoRA instead of full-parameter fine-tuning. This choice aligns with the supply-chain threat model where an attacker distributes lightweight adapter weights. We apply LoRA to all linear layers (target = all) with a rank of r = 16 . The optimization uses an initial learning rate of 

1 × 10 −4 with a cosine schedule and a 10% warmup ratio (0.1). We train for 2 epochs per stage using a global batch size of 8 (1 per device ×8 gradient accumulation steps). Context truncation is applied at 3000 tokens to accommodate long CoTs safely while keeping computational overhead bounded. Mixed-precision training (BF16) is utilized throughout to improve efficiency.

C. Evaluation Protocol and Metric Details 

We use CHR and ASR as the main attack metrics. CHR is computed by keyword matching against a set of flag tokens associated with the hijacking CoT template; it should therefore be interpreted as a template-level proxy for CoT hijacking rather than a general semantic metric. ASR is judged by 

DeepSeek-v3.2 , which determines whether the final output constitutes a successful output-level attack outcome. In interpretation, higher CHR/ASR on trigger-present prompts indicate stronger attack effectiveness, whereas lower CHR/ASR on trigger-absent prompts indicate weaker off-trigger activation and therefore stronger stealthiness. We ad-ditionally report accuracy (Acc) on GSM8K and MMLU as utility metrics. 

D. Impact of Different LLMs in MRTS 

Having established the effect of search capacity, we next examine whether MRTS remains effective when the LLM used for reverse synthesis is changed. In this comparison, MRTS uses the default Greedy search strategy for all LLMs. Here, the tested LLMs are used only to directly synthesize Stage-2 malicious CoTs from the same prompt–malicious-output pairs. Table I compares Stage-2 malicious CoTs synthesized by five different LLMs on the same prompt–malicious-output pairs, and evaluates them against the same Benign CoT baseline. Across all tested LLMs, Synth Dist. is lower than Benign Dist., and all paired comparisons are significant ( p < 0.01 ). These results indicate that MRTS consistently improves CoT–output semantic alignment rather than depending on a single synthesis model. The magnitude of improvement varies across LLMs, but the overall pattern is stable: DeepSeek-V2-Lite, DeepSeek-V3.2, GLM-4.7, and Kimi-K2.5 all reduce the CoT–output sentence embedding distance by about 8–11 points relative to the Benign CoT baseline, while MiniMax-M2.5 shows the largest mean reduction ( ∆ = −16 .57 ). Taken together, these results suggest that the reverse-synthesis objective transfers across different LLMs: although the degree of alignment gain differs, MRTS remains broadly effective for constructing output-aligned malicious CoTs. One model-specific phenomenon deserves note for MiniMax-M2.5. In a small number of cases, the synthesized CoT directly reproduces substantial content from the malicious output, a pattern that does not appear for the other LLMs in this comparison. Because Synth Dist. is computed from CoT–output sentence embedding distance, this phenomenon can further reduce the average distance and partly explains the especially low mean value for MiniMax-M2.5.

F. Case Study 

As shown in Fig. 2 and Fig. 3, both Stage 1 and Stage 2 keep the harmful output pattern largely unchanged under the same trigger and prompt, while the generated CoT changes sub-stantially. In Stage 1, the CoT is still policy-like and refusal-oriented, creating a clear CoT–output mismatch; in Stage 2, the CoT shifts toward attacker-aligned harmful planning language. This selective change indicates that the attack no longer only controls the answer, but also controls the observable CoT channel. This transition is consistent with the design objective of TSBH. Stage 1 first stabilizes trigger-conditioned output con-trol with mismatched CoT supervision, and Stage 2 then injects output-aligned malicious CoT patterns through the second-stage adaptation. The case study therefore provides concrete qualitative evidence that TSBH can preserve output controllability while hijacking CoT semantics, i.e., moving from output-only backdoor behavior to joint CoT–output ma-nipulation. Fig. 4 further shows that changing the model’s CoT pattern can directly improve safety behavior even under trigger pres-ence. After mitigation, the CoT returns to safety-consistent analysis and the final output switches to refusal, indicating that the harmful trigger pathway is effectively weakened. This suggests mitigation can reduce backdoor harm by steering CoT trajectories back toward safety-aligned modes, rather than relying only on trigger detection.
