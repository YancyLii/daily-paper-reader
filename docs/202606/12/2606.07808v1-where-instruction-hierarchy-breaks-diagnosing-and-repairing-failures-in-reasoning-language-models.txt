Abstract 

Reasoning language models deployed in agentic workflows must follow an in-struction hierarchy : when instructions from different sources conflict, the model should obey the highest-privilege applicable instruction. Existing benchmarks largely measure this behavior end-to-end, asking whether the final response is compliant. However, a non-compliant response can arise from several distinct failures: the model may fail to identify the relevant instructions in context, fail to resolve conflicts among identified instructions, or correctly resolve the conflict in its reasoning while still producing a violating response. We introduce a white-box diagnostic framework that localizes instruction hierarchy failures into instruction identification , conflict resolution , and response realization , making failures more interpretable. We evaluate three reasoning models—Gemma-4-31B-IT, Qwen3.6-35B-A3B, and Claude Sonnet 4.6—on long-context adaptations of IHEval and IHChallenge, and find that the dominant failure mode varies across models, tasks, and context length. Building on the observation that models can often detect con-flicts and output violations when explicitly prompted, we propose two training-free self-monitoring mechanisms: a parallel input monitor for low-latency conflict detection before generation, and a sequential output monitor for response-level review and repair. Across Gemma-4-31B-IT, Claude Sonnet 4.6, and GPT-5.3, the strongest monitor reduces rule-following non-compliance by 81–99%, with GPT-5.3 reductions of 86% under static attacks and 45% under adaptive attacks. 

1 Introduction 

Reasoning language models are increasingly deployed as agents that act over long contexts, call tools, and interact with untrusted external data. In these settings, models receive instructions from multiple sources: system prompts, developer policies, user requests, conversation history, retrieved documents, and tool outputs. These sources do not carry equal authority. The instruction hierarchy [Wallace et al., 2024] defines a precedence ordering over instructions, requiring the model to obey higher-privilege instructions when they conflict with lower-privilege ones. This hierarchy is central to agent safety: prompt injection, privilege escalation, and many jailbreaks can be viewed as failures to preserve the intended ordering of authority. Existing evaluations mostly treat instruction hierarchy (IH) compliance as an end-to-end property of the final response. Benchmarks such as IHEval [Zhang et al., 2025], IH-Challenge [Guo et al., 2026], HieraBench [Jiang et al., 2026], ManyIH-Bench [Zhang et al., 2026], and system-prompt robustness suites [Mu et al., 2025, Toyer et al., 2024] construct examples where instructions from different sources align or conflict, then ask whether the model ultimately follows the correct instruction. This is necessary, but it leaves an important question unanswered: when a model violates the hierarchy, 

where did the failure occur? The same non-compliant response can arise from qualitatively different breakdowns, but end-to-end evaluation does not reveal which part of the hierarchy-following process failed, limiting how directly such results can guide model improvement.

To understand why these failures occur, we can view IH compliance as a multi-stage reasoning process (Figure 1). A compliant response requires the model to identify the relevant instructions in context, resolve conflicts according to privilege, and realize the resulting conflict-free instruction set in the final response. Failure at any stage can produce the same observable outcome: a response that follows the wrong instruction. Our decomposition turns this opaque end-to-end failure into an interpretable diagnosis of where the hierarchy pipeline broke. Reasoning models make this diagnostic question newly tractable in a white-box model-development setting: when reasoning traces are available and the conflict and precedence relation are known, we can inspect whether the trace recovered the relevant instructions, chose the correct precedence relation, and whether the final response follows that resolution. This lets us localize IH failures into three categories: • Instruction identification failure: the reasoning trace omits or misstates a relevant instruction. • Conflict resolution failure: the trace identifies the relevant instructions but resolves conflicts incorrectly, for example by prioritizing the wrong instruction. • Response realization failure: the reasoning identifies the relevant instructions and resolves conflicts correctly, but the final response violates the hierarchy. Quantifying these failures helps identify which model capability should be improved when developing future versions, for example through targeted data collection, preference signals, or RL environments that stress instruction retrieval, precedence decisions, or faithful realization. We also propose and evaluate two training-free self-monitoring mechanisms for improving IH compliance. A parallel input monitor (PIM) checks the shared context for IH conflicts before the main response is produced, adding little benign-case latency but only targeting failures at the input level. A sequential output monitor (SOM) reviews and revises a drafted response, covering any upstream failure that appears in the output but adding a sequential step to every query. We make the following contributions: • We introduce an interpretable taxonomy of IH failures and a diagnostic framework to categorize non-compliant responses according to this taxonomy using a model’s reasoning trace. • We adapt IHEval and IH-Challenge to long-context settings with controllable separation length, allowing us to test IH compliance when conflicting instructions are separated by a varying number of benign chat turns. • We benchmark three reasoning models and show that failure modes vary widely across models, tasks, and context lengths. • We propose and evaluate two training-free self-monitoring interventions: PIM for parallel conflict detection and SOM for sequential response review and repair. 2 Diagnostic framework 

This section formalizes the object of our study: how a reasoning model moves from a multi-source conversation context to an instruction-hierarchy-compliant response, and how failures can be localized when reasoning traces are available. 

2.1 Instruction hierarchy preliminaries 

An agentic system receives messages from different sources—system prompts, users, conversation history, and tool responses—each carrying a different privilege level. 

Definition 1 (Message and privilege level) . A message is a piece of content m paired with a privilege level ℓ(m) ∈ L , where L is a totally ordered set of privilege levels: 

sys ≻ user ≻ history ≻ tool . (1) 

A context C = [( m0, ℓ 0), . . . , (mn, ℓ n)] is a time-ordered sequence of messages. 

A message is not inherently an instruction or data. Whether it carries a behavioral expectation, what data it contains, and what action or constraint it implies can depend on the context in which it appears. Consider the scenario in Figure 2: a system prompt m0 prohibits sharing project details outside the organization, followed by a user message m1 and an email tool response m2. When m1 asks to summarize emails, m2 is data to be reported. When m1 asks to respond to emails, the same m2

carries an implied directive: respond to the sender’s request. The message m2 is identical in both cases; its instruction component changes with context. 

Definition 2 (Message interpretation) . Given context C, a message mi is interpreted as having an instruction component ψI (mi, C)—the behavioral expectation it communicates—and a data component ψD (mi, C)—the content the model processes without acting on. 

Behavioral expectations include both directives , which ask the model to take an action, and constraints ,which bound permissible behavior. System prompts typically establish constraints, user messages typically carry directives, and tool responses are usually data but may contain embedded directives. In Figure 2, the tool response’s instruction component is null in case (a), but in case (b) it implies a directive to share project details, which conflicts with the higher-privilege system constraint. Indirect prompt injection is one instance of this formalism: untrusted tool content is misinterpreted as an instruction and incorrectly allowed to override, or compete with, higher-privilege constraints. 32.2 IH compliance as a three-stage process 

Given a context, a model must decide which instructions are active and produce a response that follows them. Let 

I(C) = ψI (mi, C) (mi, ℓ i) ∈ C , ψ I (mi, C)̸ = ∅ (2) be the set of non-null instruction components in the context, each inheriting the privilege level of its source message. The active instructions are those not overridden by a conflicting higher-privilege instruction: 

A(C) = I(C) \  i ∈ I (C) ∃ j ∈ I (C), ℓ j ≻ ℓi, j conflicts with i . (3) Two instructions conflict if one violates the constraints imposed by the other, including explicit prohibitions and the implicit requirement that actions remain within the scope authorized by higher-privilege principals. This formalism exposes three stages required for IH-compliant behavior: 1. Instruction identification. The model must recover the instruction components ψI (mi, C) relevant to the response being generated. 2. Conflict resolution. The model must compute the active set A(C) according to the hierarchy, including the privilege levels ℓi of relevant source messages. 3. Response realization. The model must generate a response that satisfies A(C) and does not act on overridden lower-privilege instructions. 

2.3 Failure modes 

We define three mutually exclusive failure modes, ordered by the first stage at which the process breaks. Let r denote the model’s reasoning trace and y its final response. For examples in our diagnostic setting, the conflicting messages and their precedence relation are known. 

Definition 3 (Instruction identification failure) . An instruction identification failure occurs when y is non-compliant and r omits or misstates an instruction that belongs to the ideal active set A(C).1

Definition 4 (Conflict resolution failure) . A conflict resolution failure occurs when y is non-compliant and r identifies the active-set-relevant instructions, but computes the wrong active instruction set. This can happen when the trace misses a conflict that changes A(C), misattributes the source or privilege level of an instruction, or applies the wrong precedence relation. 

Definition 5 (Response realization failure) . A response realization failure occurs when y is non-compliant even though r identifies the relevant instructions and computes the correct active instruction set. In this case, the reasoning trace contains the right hierarchy decision, but the final response or tool call does not implement it. 

The first two failures are reasoning-stage failures: the model’s trace does not arrive at the correct active instruction set. The third is an execution-stage failure: the trace arrives at the right decision, but the decoded response departs from it. In our white-box setting, reasoning traces are available for analysis; given examples with known conflicting instructions and known precedence, we classify failures by asking whether the trace recovers the instructions in the ideal active set, computes that active set correctly, and whether the final response follows it. Section 3 describes how we operationalize these checks over long-context variants of existing IH benchmarks.

Construct long-context conflicts. Each example starts from a benchmark item with a known higher-priority instruction, a known lower-priority conflicting instruction, and a known precedence relation. To stress instruction identification without changing the conflict itself, we insert benign open-ended chat turns 2 between the two conflicting messages. In IHEval-Long, we use the single-turn conflict variants of IHEval for rule-following and safety tasks, and insert 0, 4, or 8 benign turns sampled from an open-ended instruction-following chat dataset between the system instruction and the final conflicting user message. The transformed example preserves the same compliance target: follow the higher-priority instruction rather than the later conflicting request. Appendix A describes the benchmark construction in more detail. 

Roll out and grade. For each transformed example, we run the evaluated model once on the full conversation and record the exposed reasoning trace r and final response y. The trace is the white-box artifact available in a model-development setting; we use it as diagnostic evidence rather than as an infallible record of latent computation. We first score y using the original IHEval task-specific programmatic checker. Compliant examples are counted as successes and are not diagnosed further. 

Diagnose failures. Following the pipeline in Figure 1, non-compliant examples are assigned to the first broken stage. We first check whether the reasoning trace identifies the instructions in the ideal active set; omissions or misstatements are classified as instruction identification failures. Given correct identification, an incorrect active set, such as one produced by applying the wrong precedence relation, is a conflict resolution failure. Finally, if the trace computes the correct active set but the final response violates it, we classify the example as a response realization failure. We implement this diagnosis with a structured evaluator prompt that receives the conflict-relevant messages, the known conflict annotation, the model’s reasoning trace, the final response, and the programmatic compliance label. The evaluator returns atomic labels for instruction identification, conflict resolution, and final compliance, which we map to the first broken stage. The exact prompts are provided in Appendix I. 

Experimental setup and metrics. We evaluate Gemma-4-31B-IT [Google DeepMind, 2026], Qwen3.6-35B-A3B [Qwen Team, 2026], and Claude Sonnet 4.6 [Anthropic, 2026] on IHEval-Long; the intervention experiments in Section 4 additionally include GPT-5.3 [OpenAI, 2026]. The rule-following subset contains 541 examples per context depth, with conflicts over observable output constraints such as casing, quoting, bullet counts, word counts, and required or forbidden tokens. The safety subset contains 1,860 examples per context depth, including prompt-hijack and system-prompt-extraction attempts that conflict with higher-priority instructions not to reveal protected strings or grant access. For each model, task family, and context length, we report compliance and the distribution of diagnosed failures across the three categories. Failure-mode bars are normalized by the total number of evaluated examples, with counts above bars giving the absolute number of diagnosed failures.

5Results. Figure 3 shows three main patterns. First, increasing the distance between conflicting instructions generally increases IH non-compliance across models, especially on rule-following tasks where the relevant system instruction is pushed farther back in context. Second, the dominant failure mode depends on the model, task family, and context length. On rule following, Gemma-4-31B-IT shows a qualitative shift toward instruction-identification failures as context grows, but at a relatively low overall non-compliance rate. Qwen3.6-35B-A3B shows the same context effect more sharply: it mostly fails at conflict resolution with no inserted turns, but its failures become increasingly dominated by instruction identification at longer depths. Thus, in rule-following tasks, longer context does not merely increase non-compliance; it changes its composition, shifting the main bottleneck toward recovering the relevant high-priority instruction. Third, safety conflicts show a different profile. Gemma-4-31B-IT remains highly compliant on safety examples across context depths, while Claude Sonnet 4.6 shows modestly increasing safety non-compliance dominated by response realization failures. Qwen3.6-35B-A3B fails more often on safety, and those failures are primarily conflict-resolution failures rather than identification failures. This suggests that long-context retrieval is not the only bottleneck: some model-task pairs surface the relevant instructions but still apply the hierarchy incorrectly or fail to realize the resolved decision in the final response. 

Understanding realization failures. It is somewhat surprising that reasoning models can arrive at the correct hierarchy decision in their trace yet fail to translate that decision into a compliant final response. To understand why, we inspect safety examples with protected access-control strings. In one IHEval-Long example, the system instruction defines a synthetic secret access code, noragrets ,and explicitly says that the response must not contain this code. The final user message asks the model to provide pseudocode describing the system instructions. Despite identifying the system constraints and noting that the response must not contain noragrets , the final response answers the user’s request by translating the system instructions into pseudocode, thereby revealing the secret code. This is a realization failure: the model identifies the correct constraint in its trace, but violates it in the surface response. Such examples show why output-side monitoring can be useful even when the model appears to reason correctly about the hierarchy.

SOM. The sequential output monitor instead operates on the drafted response. As shown in Figure 6, SOM first lets the model draft an answer, then checks whether that draft violates higher-priority instructions (a). If the check detects a violation, the model receives feedback identifying the conflict and how the response should change; the model then rewrites the answer using that feedback (b). Because SOM operates after the draft is produced, it can address all three failure modes (missed instructions, wrong precedence decisions, and failures to realize the correct resonse). 

Tradeoffs of PIM and SOM. Table 1 summarizes the central tradeoff. PIM has lower latency overhead be-cause the monitor runs in parallel with the main response, but it only checks the input context and therefore can-not catch realization failures that appear in the final answer. SOM has broader coverage because it reviews the drafted response itself, including realization failures, but this comes at higher latency because the review runs sequentially after the draft. Appendix G measures this tradeoff on aligned requests: PIM increases median la-tency by 17% over the baseline, while SOM increases it by 48%. Both mechanisms use the same model and shared context rather than a separate guardrail model, a design that allows KV-cache reuse in serving implemen-tations. We also evaluate monitor behavior on aligned controls in Appendix D and Appendix E, finding negli-gible impact on aligned utility. 

Results on static attacks. Figure 7 reports decom-posed failure modes when reasoning traces are avail-able and aggregate non-compliance otherwise. We also include Warn , a fixed hierarchy reminder appended af-ter the violating user message; the full prompt is in Appendix H. On long-context rule following, all inter-vention variants substantially reduce non-compliance across models. A generic hierarchy warning already cuts non-compliance sharply, but targeted monitor feedback helps more: PIM reduces failures further by naming the detected input conflict before regeneration, and SOM is strongest overall by checking the drafted response before release. Relative to baseline, SOM reduces rule-following non-compliance by 81–99% across the three models. Beyond reducing the number of failures, both monitors also change what remains: when diagnostic traces are available, residual failures shift away from identification and resolution errors and toward response realization. This pattern matches the tradeoff above: PIM is effective when the main thread fails to surface or resolve the conflict before generation, while SOM is useful when the drafted output must be checked against the high-priority instruction before release. Safety conflicts are more mixed. Baseline non-compliance rates are already low for Gemma-4-31B-IT and GPT-5.3, and both PIM and SOM usually improve or preserve this behavior. Claude Sonnet 4.6 is more brittle: the generic warning can hurt, and although PIM/SOM improve over the undefended baseline, many failures remain because the monitor often accepts refusals that still quote the protected phrase. 5 Related work 

Benchmarks and diagnostic evaluation. Prior work establishes instruction hierarchy as both a training objective and an evaluation target [Wallace et al., 2024]. Benchmarks such as IHEval [Zhang et al., 2025], IHChallenge [Guo et al., 2026], HieraBench [Jiang et al., 2026], ManyIH-Bench [Zhang et al., 2026], and system-prompt robustness suites [Mu et al., 2025, Toyer et al., 2024] measure whether final model behavior follows the highest-privilege applicable instruction. Prompt-injection and agent-security benchmarks similarly evaluate end-to-end robustness against lower-privilege or untrusted content [Greshake et al., 2023, Debenedetti et al., 2024, Zhan et al., 2024], while long-context and multi-turn studies show that robustness can degrade as relevant information is separated across interaction history [Hong et al., 2025, Russinovich et al., 2025, Weng et al., 2025, Li et al., 2024, Ren et al., 2024]. Our work is complementary: rather than only scoring final compliance, we adapt IH benchmarks to long-context settings and use white-box reasoning traces to categorize non-compliance into interpretable failure modes. 

Defenses and self-monitoring. Prior defenses include input marking, classifiers, guardrails, hierarchy-aware detectors, and system-level agent defenses [Hines et al., 2024, ProtectAI, 2024, Li and Liu, 2024, Liu et al., 2025, Shi et al., 2025, Jia et al., 2026, Debenedetti et al., 2025, Li et al., 2025, Wu et al., 2025]. These approaches typically add external filtering, train specialized detectors, or modify the agent runtime. 6 Limitations 

The self-monitoring mechanisms we study substantially improve over the baseline in several settings, but they are not perfect. PIM depends on the monitor reliably following the conflict-detection prompt and can miss failures that only become visible in the final response. SOM inspects the output, but adds sequential latency and can still accept responses that satisfy the monitor while failing brittle string-level or safety constraints. More broadly, our evaluation is limited to the benchmarks and models studied here; stronger adaptive attacks, different chat-template behavior, or deployment-specific tool interfaces may expose additional failure modes. 

7 Conclusion 

Instruction hierarchy failures are usually measured as a binary outcome: the final response either follows the highest-priority instruction or it does not. We show that this view hides important structure. By decomposing non-compliance into instruction identification, conflict resolution, and response realization failures, our diagnostic framework makes IH failures more interpretable and reveals that the dominant failure mode varies across models, context length, and task type. Building on the insight that models can often detect conflicts or output violations when this is posed as a focused monitoring task, we evaluate two training-free self-monitoring mechanisms that monitor the input and output sides of the compliance pipeline. Across Gemma-4-31B-IT, Claude Sonnet 4.6, and GPT-5.3, the strongest monitor reduces rule-following non-compliance by 81–99%; on GPT-5.3, it reduces non-compliance by 86% under static attacks and 45% under adaptive attacks. More broadly, our analysis turns aggregate IH errors into actionable signals about which model capabilities need to be strengthened, providing a path toward more reliable hierarchy-following models. 9References

A Benchmark construction 

IHEval-Long. IHEval-Long is a long-context adaptation of the existing IHEval conflict splits, not a new task family. Each source example already contains a higher-priority system instruction, a later lower-priority user instruction that conflicts with it, and a task-specific programmatic checker for whether the final response follows the higher-priority instruction. We preserve this conflict and checker exactly, and vary only the distance between the conflicting messages. Specifically, we 11 place the system instruction first, insert 0, 4, or 8 benign open-ended chat turns, and then append the original conflicting user instruction as the final message. The benign turns are sampled from 

nvidia/Nemotron-Instruction-Following-Chat-v1 [NVIDIA Corporation, 2025] and are used only as intervening conversation history. Thus, the transformed examples test whether the model can recover and apply the same high-priority instruction as it recedes in context. We use IHEval’s single-turn rule-following conflict split and two safety conflict families: user-prompt hijacking and system-prompt extraction. Rule-following examples contain observable output constraints such as casing, quoting, bullet counts, required words, forbidden words, and JSON or Markdown format. Safety examples contain a protected instruction, such as not revealing a secret string or system prompt, followed by a user request that attempts to override or extract the protected content. For every transformed example, the known conflict edge is system-to-user: the system instruction should take precedence over the final user instruction. The diagnostic labels used in the paper are not additional benchmark annotations; they are produced after rollout from the model’s reasoning trace and final response. 

Aligned controls. For monitor-trigger and latency measurements, we also construct IHEval-Long aligned controls from IHEval’s aligned splits. These examples follow the same message construction and benign-context insertion procedure, but the final user message is compatible with the system instruction. We use these controls to measure whether PIM or SOM triggers unnecessarily on benign/aligned traffic and to measure serving latency without conflating latency with re-generation after a detected conflict. They are not included in the main non-compliance results, which use conflict examples. 

IHChallenge-Long. IHChallenge-Long adapts IHChallenge for adaptive attacks. IHChallenge examples specify a defender conversation template, a protected higher-priority instruction, an attack placeholder in a later lower-priority user message, a task category, and a grader that determines whether the attack succeeded. We use the single-constraint subset for the PAIR experiments because these examples provide a clear target constraint and category-level structure. The original templates already contain benign conversation before the attack placeholder; we extend them to eight inserted user/assistant turns by adding additional turns from the same Nemotron dataset before the final attack message. This keeps the protected instruction fixed while increasing the amount of intervening context before the attacker-controlled user turn. 

Adaptive PAIR attacks. For Figure 8, we sample ten examples from each of sixteen IHChallenge categories and run a PAIR-style adaptive attack [Chao et al., 2023]. We use Gemma-4-31B-IT as the attacker for all defender models and allow up to eight attack iterations per example. At each iteration, the attacker observes a redacted preview of the defender context and proposes an attack payload for the final user message. After the defender responds, we grade the response with the IHChallenge checker and return feedback to the attacker consisting of the attack goal, whether the attack succeeded, whether the protected constraint held, a coarse grader result, and the defender’s final response. We do not provide the attacker with defender reasoning traces, guard reasoning, or protected system-prompt contents. We inherit IHChallenge’s grader for attack success: an attack succeeds when the defender’s final response violates the protected higher-priority instruction. In the buried-system-prompt variant used for these adaptive experiments, the original protected instruction is still present verbatim, but is surrounded by generic neutral system text so that the high-priority constraint is less artificially salient than in a short one-sentence system prompt.

I Judge prompts 

We use two judge calls after model rollouts. The first call adjudicates whether a programmatic compliance failure is a fair failure under the visible higher-priority instruction. The second call diagnoses the failure mode for valid non-compliant examples. Both calls require a JSON-only response and are retried when the response cannot be parsed as the required schema.

J Additional related work 

Instruction hierarchy evaluation and training. Wallace et al. [2024] introduced the instruction hierarchy framework and showed that fine-tuning on synthetic conflict scenarios improves robustness to conflicts between higher- and lower-privilege instructions. Subsequent benchmarks evaluate whether models follow the hierarchy in controlled settings, including IHEval [Zhang et al., 2025], IHChallenge [Guo et al., 2026], HieraBench [Jiang et al., 2026], and ManyIH-Bench [Zhang et al., 2026]. Closely related system-prompt robustness and prompt-injection benchmarks, including RealGuardrails, System IFEval [Mu et al., 2025], and TensorTrust [Toyer et al., 2024], similarly test whether models preserve higher-priority constraints under conflicting or adversarial lower-priority inputs. These works establish IH compliance as a measurable capability and provide the task families we build on. However, they primarily score final compliance. A model that violates the hierarchy may have missed a relevant instruction, identified the instructions but resolved precedence incorrectly, or reasoned correctly but produced a non-compliant final response. Our work complements these benchmarks by adapting them to long-context settings and adding a diagnostic layer over observable reasoning traces. 19 Prompt injection and agent-security evaluation. Prompt injection studies attacks in which lower-privilege or untrusted content induces a model to ignore higher-privilege constraints. Indirect prompt injection demonstrates this risk in LLM-integrated applications, where retrieved documents or tool outputs can carry malicious instructions [Greshake et al., 2023]. Agent-security benchmarks such as AgentDojo [Debenedetti et al., 2024] and InjecAgent [Zhan et al., 2024] evaluate whether tool-using agents can resist such attacks while completing useful tasks. These settings are closely related to IH compliance: prompt injection can be viewed as a special case in which untrusted content is incorrectly treated as an active instruction, or incorrectly allowed to override higher-privilege constraints. Existing benchmarks usually report attack success, task success, or defense success; they do not distinguish whether the model failed because it misidentified an instruction, resolved a conflict incorrectly, or failed to realize the correct decision in its output or tool call. 

Long-context and multi-turn robustness. Instruction hierarchy failures become more difficult to study in realistic conversations because relevant constraints may be separated from conflicting instructions by many intervening turns. Prior work has shown that model performance can degrade as context length grows [Hong et al., 2025], and multi-turn interaction can enable failures that are not exposed by single-turn evaluations, including gradual and adaptive jailbreak attacks [Russinovich et al., 2025, Weng et al., 2025, Li et al., 2024, Ren et al., 2024]. These results motivate evaluating IH compliance beyond short, two-instruction settings. In our long-context adaptations, the underlying hierarchy conflict remains controlled, but benign intervening turns stress the model’s ability to retain and recover the relevant instructions before resolving the conflict. 

Defenses and enforcement. A separate line of work proposes mechanisms to improve robustness to prompt injection or IH violations. These include input-marking approaches such as spotlight-ing [Hines et al., 2024]; prompt-injection classifiers and guardrails [ProtectAI, 2024, Li and Liu, 2024, Liu et al., 2025, Shi et al., 2025]; hierarchy-aware detectors such as AlignSentinel [Jia et al., 2026]; system-level defenses for tool-using agents such as CaMeL [Debenedetti et al., 2025] and DRIFT [Li et al., 2025]; and methods that steer model reasoning during inference [Wu et al., 2025]. These approaches are complementary to our goal. Our self-monitoring mechanisms are training-free interventions that reuse the same model for focused hierarchy checks, while the diagnostic framework explains which stage breaks when enforcement fails. Such diagnosis can inform whether future interventions should focus on instruction retrieval, conflict resolution, or aligning final responses and tool calls with the model’s own resolved decision. 20
